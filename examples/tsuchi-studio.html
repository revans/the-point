Tsuchi Studio

Shigaraki clay  ·  Wood-fired  ·  Higashiyama, Kyoto

One potter. One clay source. One kiln.

Every piece made at Tsuchi Studio begins as Shigaraki clay — iron-rich stoneware dug from Shiga Prefecture, 60 kilometers northeast of this studio. The clay is thrown by a single potter who has worked at the same wheel for 22 years in Higashiyama, Kyoto's oldest craft district. The work is functional: bowls, cups, small plates. Nothing is decorative.

Finished pieces are fired in an anagama — a single-chamber wood-burning kiln built into the hillside behind the studio. Each firing runs for four continuous days and cannot be stopped once begun. The ash from the pine fuel settles on the clay during firing, melting into the surface where it lands. No two pieces carry the same glaze. The kiln decides that.

Material

Shigaraki clay, Shiga Prefecture

Shigaraki is one of Japan's six ancient kiln sites. The clay is coarse-grained, high in iron and feldspar, and fires at temperatures most ceramic bodies cannot tolerate. At 1,280°C it vitrifies — becomes dense, non-porous, and hard enough to hold water without any added glaze.

In the finished piece, the iron content appears as dark speckling through the clay body. Where ash from the kiln settles and melts, the surface shifts from the natural warm grey of the stone to streaks of green, amber, and grey-white — colors produced by the specific minerals in pine ash combining with the iron in Shigaraki clay. This surface cannot be replicated in an electric kiln.

You can name the person who made it, the mountain the clay came from, and the wood that fired it.
