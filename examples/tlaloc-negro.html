Tlāloc Negro

17°04′N 96°22′W  ·  2,400 m  ·  Est. 1952

01

Harvest

Wild agave espadin, 12–14 years mature, cut by hand at 2,400 meters. Each plant selected by the palenquero — no mechanical harvest.

02

Roasting

Underground pit lined with river stones. Hardwood fire burns 72 hours. The smoke enters the piña — this is where the mezcal begins.

03

Crushing

Stone tahona wheel, mule-drawn, turns at the pace it turns. The fibers separate slowly. No mechanical shredder has been on this property.

04

Fermentation

Open wooden vats, ambient yeast only. Eight to twelve days, depending on temperature and phase of moon. No inoculants. No added enzymes.

05

Distillation

Clay pot still, twice distilled. The heads and tails are cut by taste and smell, not by instrument. The cut has been the same since 1952.

17°04′17″N 96°22′43″W 2,400 m.s.n.m. San Baltazar Guelavila Sierra Juárez, Oaxaca Agave angustifolia Haw. Est. 1952 — Familia Vásquez Cruz 400 botellas por cosecha

The palenque sits at the edge of a milpa that has been worked by the Vásquez Cruz family since before the current distillery was built. The agave here grows slowly. At 2,400 meters the nights are cold and the soil is thin — the plant stores more sugar because it has to work harder. Rogelio Vásquez Cruz learned the cut from his father, who learned it from his. There is no written recipe. The sensory knowledge is what passes down: when the smoke is enough, when the fermentation is ready, where to draw the line between the heart and the tail. Each bottle carries a lot number and a hand-written sequence. When 400 are gone, they are gone. The next harvest begins when the agave says it is ready.
