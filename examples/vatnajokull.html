Ex terra glacialis

What the glacier gives, one season at a time.

Concentrated botanical extracts hand-harvested from Iceland's Vatnajökull region. For bar programs and kitchen work that require something the supply chain cannot provide.

The Practice

Each extract begins with a walk. Icelandic moss grows on lava fields that have not been tilled. Crowberries ripen in a window measured in days, not weeks. Silver birch catkins fall once a year. The Vatnajökull region sits at the intersection of glacial meltwater and volcanic mineral deposits — a soil chemistry found nowhere else. What comes out of the bottle arrived there on foot, in season, by one person who knew what to look for.

The Extracts
01

Cetraria islandica

Icelandic Moss

Collected from open lava fields above 400 metres, where the lichen grows undisturbed in conditions too harsh for most other vegetation. The extract is bitter and mineral — the kind of bitterness that holds its shape in a long drink or a broth reduction. Bartenders use it where they want a Nordic herbaceous note that reads as green without reading as herb. Chefs use it to anchor broths that need weight without sweetness.

Bitter mineral Dry forest floor Cold stone
02

Empetrum nigrum

Crowberry

The harvest window is late August to early September, when the berries reach peak sugar-acid tension just before the first frost breaks them down. Crowberry extract is dark and tart with a faint resinous back note from the shrub itself. It behaves differently from any cultivated berry — less sweet, more complex, with an astringency that makes it work against fat. In a cocktail it functions as a modifier, not a feature. In a sauce it tightens rather than sweetens.

Dark tart fruit Resinous shrub Iron-mineral finish
03

Betula pubescens

Silver Birch Catkin

Catkins are gathered in early spring — a brief window before full leaf break when the aromatic oils in the male flower are at their most concentrated. The extract carries the green-woody character of a birch forest in April: sap-bright, slightly medicinal, with a clean finish that doesn't linger into bitterness. It lifts aquavit-based builds without competing with them. In the kitchen it works alongside white fish, young vegetables, and any preparation where a Nordic register is wanted without the weight of smoke or cured notes.

Green wood Spring sap Light medicinal
Provenance

Europe's largest glacier. One harvest season.

Vatnajökull covers roughly eight percent of Iceland's landmass. Its outermost edges are volcanic highlands — a landscape shaped by the collision of glacial cold and geothermal heat. The mineral content of the soil is unlike anything in managed agricultural land. Nothing here is cultivated. The species that thrive do so because the conditions selected for them over thousands of years. That specificity is what you taste.
