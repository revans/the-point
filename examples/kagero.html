The Room

Eight rooms.
No two alike.

The floors are tatami. Cedar posts carry the ceiling. The shoji screens face the valley, and in the morning the light through the paper is the color of the paper. A futon is laid at dusk. The low table holds a ceramic teapot and two cups.

There is no television. There is no lock on the door. The key to the room is the room itself — the knowledge that no one else is in the inn who should not be there.

The bath faces east. In winter, you watch the sun come up over the cedar ridge from the water.

The Meal

Dinner is
at seven.

The innkeeper prepares kaiseki. The menu changes with the week, not the season — because the mountain changes week by week. In December: river fish from the Miya, burdock from the valley floor, a broth that has been reducing since morning.

The lacquerware is the inn's own, some of it older than the guest's grandparents. It is not displayed. It is used.

The Year
at Kagero

  • Winter December — March. The inn is open. Snow from the first week of December. The rotenburo steams against the cold. Reservations are required; the inn does not take walk-ins.
  • Spring Closed. The Mori family rests the building. The tatami is re-laid. The wood is oiled. Some things cannot happen while guests are present.
  • Summer Closed. The mountain is beautiful in summer. The inn is not here to show it to you.
  • Autumn Closed. Reservations for the following winter open in March. The waiting list forms quietly. There is no reminder system.
