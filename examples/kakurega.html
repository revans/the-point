豆腐の隠れ家 · Yamashina-ku, Kyoto

Kinugoshi tofu, stone-ground before sunrise, for the twelve kaiseki kitchens that carry it.

Tōfu no Kakurega has produced kinugoshi tofu in Yamashina-ku, Kyoto for four generations. Every block begins with Tamba kurodaizu black soybeans — a variety grown in Hyogo Prefecture whose higher fat content produces a soymilk dense enough to set without pressing. We draw water exclusively from the Fushimi underground aquifer, unchanged in mineral composition since the third generation standardized the source. The tofu reaches twelve kaiseki restaurants in Kyoto and Osaka. We supply no retail. Relationships are wholesale only.

The Grind — Ishiusu

Stone-ground between 3 and 5 a.m., before the soymilk loses its temperature window.

The ishiusu — a paired granite millstone rotated by hand — grinds the soaked kurodaizu at a speed that generates almost no heat. Mechanical milling at production scale reaches temperatures that denature the proteins responsible for clean coagulation; the soymilk sets unevenly, and the resulting texture in kinugoshi is detectably grainy at the surface. Stone grinding keeps the soymilk below 15°C through the entire process. We begin each morning before sunrise because the Fushimi water, drawn at night, holds its coldest temperature in the pre-dawn hours. The window from grind to coagulation is less than ninety minutes. What enters the mold is the product of that window.

The Set — Kinugoshi

Silk-strained, never pressed — the structure holds because nothing was forced out of it.

Kinugoshi is coagulated directly in the mold. The soymilk is poured at 75°C into cedar frames lined with silk cloth, nigari added at measured concentration, then covered and left undisturbed for forty minutes. There is no pressing. Momen — the cotton-pressed variety — expels moisture by force, which compacts protein structure and produces a firmer, drier result. Kinugoshi retains the full hydration of the original soymilk: the curd holds itself together through coagulation alone. In the hands of a kaiseki chef, this means the block cuts without crumbling, releases cleanly from a spoon, and carries the flavor of the soybean rather than the flavor of process. The cedar mold contributes a faint woodiness that dissipates by service; the silk cloth leaves no mark on the surface.

Supply & Handling

Daily production. Morning delivery. Twelve current accounts across Kyoto and Osaka.

Production runs six mornings a week. Delivery reaches affiliated kitchens between 8 and 10 a.m., the tofu set that morning. The twelve current accounts represent the full volume we can supply without altering the production process; new wholesale relationships are accepted when existing demand allows. Receiving: kinugoshi should be stored submerged in cold water at 4°C and used within 48 hours of delivery. It does not improve with time. If your program requires tofu two or more days out from delivery, kinugoshi at this moisture level is not the right fit — momen tolerates that handling better. Serving: remove from water, drain on a clean cloth for three minutes, do not rinse with warm water. The block reaches its best texture slightly below room temperature, not cold from refrigeration.

Wholesale Inquiry

For kaiseki and kappo kitchens in Kyoto, Osaka, and Nara.

This form is for chefs and purchasing managers considering a wholesale relationship. We review new inquiries when production capacity allows — currently that is infrequent. We will respond within two weeks. If we cannot accommodate your kitchen at this time, we will say so directly. We do not maintain a waiting list. What helps us evaluate an inquiry: a clear description of how kinugoshi functions in your program, your expected weekly volume, and the season or menu context you have in mind.
