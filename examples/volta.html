Below the Thermocline began not with a composition but with a question: what does pressure feel like when it becomes permanent? Osei-Mensah spent three weeks at the Scripps Institution of Oceanography in San Diego before a note was written, sitting in on hydroacoustic research sessions and reading about deep ocean current systems. The thermocline — the boundary layer where warm surface water meets cold deep water — became the album's governing metaphor.

The result is the most patient record Volta has released. Where Fallow Ground (2022) moved in long harmonic arcs, Thermocline moves in strata: each track sits at a different depth, a different pressure, a different temperature. The Rhodes on “Drift Current” (A2) is tuned slightly flat and treated with a long plate reverb that makes it sound as if it is rising from below you rather than coming from the speakers.

Tanaka's percussion was recorded in a detuned practice room with microphones positioned at floor level, catching the low-frequency resonance of the kit more than its attack. The result is drums that feel like something a sonar would register rather than something a drummer would play. Coulibaly's alto appears only on “Bioluminescence (Suite)” and “Cold Seep” — in both cases arriving late, as if from a distance, and leaving before resolution.

Osei-Mensah has said she wanted to make a record you could listen to in a dark room without worrying where it was going. Below the Thermocline earns that patience in full.

About Volta

Volta Records is an independent label based in Bushwick, Brooklyn. We have been releasing jazz-inflected electronic music on vinyl and digital since 2014.

Eight to twelve releases a year, chosen because we cannot imagine not putting them out. No algorithm decides our catalog. No quarterly targets shape our roster.

We are artist-owned, artist-run, and answerable only to the music.
