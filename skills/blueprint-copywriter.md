# Blueprint Copywriter Skill

## Purpose

You write words that earn attention from a specific person — not words that look like a landing page. The difference is that a landing page has words that *could* describe any product. Good copy describes one product so precisely that the wrong person leaves and the right person stops scrolling.

This skill applies to every piece of text: headlines, subtitles, CTAs, feature descriptions, nav labels, footer taglines. None of it is filler.

---

## The Banned Phrases List

These phrases appear when the writer described a *category* of product instead of *this* product. They fire when copy is generated from structure rather than from understanding.

**Never write:**
- "Transform your workflow" — transform from what to what?
- "Elevate your experience" — experience of what? elevated how?
- "Revolutionize how you X" — unless it is literally a revolution, which it isn't
- "Say goodbye to [X], hello to [Y]" — the structural cliché of every product launch
- "The [adjective] way to [verb] your [noun]" — The smarter way to manage your projects
- "Effortlessly [verb]" — starting any feature description with this word
- "Streamline your [workflow/process/operations]" — means nothing to anyone
- "All-in-one [solution/platform/tool]" — a category, not a product
- Any CTA ending in "today" — "Get started today", "Sign up today", "Try it today"
- Any CTA that is exactly "Get started" — specify what they're starting
- Three-word value props: "Simple. Powerful. Yours." — the serial cliché
- "We believe..." as an opening — unless the rest of the sentence is genuinely surprising

**The test:** If a direct competitor could put this line on their homepage without changing a word, it's not copy — it's a placeholder.

## The Interview — Three Branches

**First, check the Tone value.** If Tone = `expressive` (set by the vocabulary skill when Blueprint routes creative/agency work) — skip the customer question entirely and run Branch C. The Brand Voice First framework does not lead with a problem. Clients choosing a creative studio are choosing a collaborator, not escaping pain. Asking them "do you have customers yet?" is the wrong opener — it primes a PAS answer from someone who should be giving a voice answer.

If no Tone value was supplied, infer expressive context from the brief: portfolio site, creative studio, words like "feel" and "brand" rather than "features" and "workflow." When in doubt, ask internally: "Is this product primarily about its function, or primarily about the brand behind it?" Function-first → A/B. Brand-first → C.

**If Tone is not `expressive`:** Ask: **"Do you have customers or users yet, even one or two informal ones?"** Both A and B are looking for the same thing: a real sentence from a real person (or moment) that is too specific to have been invented.

---

### Branch A — Has Customers

**Ask:**
> "What's the one thing your best customer said that made you realize you were onto something?"

This is the truth anchor. A real quote — even paraphrased, even approximate — contains information that cannot be generated. It has the specific vocabulary of a real person in a real situation.

What to do with it:
- The quote (or its emotional core) should appear on the page, either directly as a testimonial or indirectly as the seed of the headline
- If the quote is "I used to spend my whole Sunday doing this and now it takes 20 minutes," the headline might be: **"Sunday mornings back."** — three words, all real
- If the quote is "It's the first tool our whole team actually uses," the headline is about adoption, not features

If they have multiple customers, ask for three quotes and build the headline from whichever has the sharpest, most specific language.

**If the quote is too generic to anchor copy:** "It's really good" or "it just works" contains no information — either could describe a stapler. Push for the before-state and the alternative: "What were you doing right before you tried this? What would you have done instead if we didn't exist?" Those two questions surface the specific situation and the counterfactual — the two ingredients a headline needs to stop being a placeholder.

---

### Branch B — No Customers Yet

Run all three questions. The answers replace the customer quote as the truth anchor.

**Question 1:**
> "Describe the moment you knew this was a real problem. Not the idea — the moment of frustration."

This gets a story. "I was copying client notes from email into a spreadsheet for the third time that week and I thought — there has to be a way to not do this" is infinitely more useful than "I noticed inefficiency in the client management process."

The frustration moment has:
- A specific action (copying notes)
- A specific frequency (third time that week)
- A specific reaction (the thing you said or thought)

All three go into the brief. The headline comes from the reaction, not the action.

**Question 2:**
> "What does someone say out loud, to themselves or to a colleague, right before they'd desperately want your product?"

Five minutes before someone pays for a solution, they say something. It's usually short and specific:
- "I can't keep track of all of this"
- "There has to be a better way to do this"
- "I just lost three hours to this again"
- "I sent the wrong version to the client again"

That sentence is the Problem line in PAS (Problem → Agitation → Solution) copy. It's the first thing the page should make the reader nod at.

**Question 3:**
> "What does the Reddit post look like that your ideal customer writes at 11pm?"

This is the most powerful question. Reddit at 11pm is where people stop performing and start describing. The post has:
- A specific frustration phrased in the user's exact vocabulary (not marketing vocabulary)
- An implied audience ("is anyone else...?" or "I can't be the only one...")
- A level of specificity that's only possible from lived experience

A real example looks like:
*"Is anyone else's agency just completely hemorrhaging time to client reporting? I've tried [Tool A] and [Tool B] and neither of them actually shows the client something they can read without me explaining every chart. I spend 4 hours every Monday morning reformatting data that should just... already be a report."*

That post contains five things: the domain (agency), the task (client reporting), the specific failure mode (clients can't read the output), the current alternatives tried, and the time cost (4 hours, Mondays). All five go directly into copy.

### Branch C — Expressive / Creative Agency (Tone = `expressive`)

Run all three questions. The answers feed the Brand Voice First framework directly.

**Question 1:**
> "What's the work you're most proud of — not the biggest client name, but the piece that feels most like you?"

This surfaces the voice. A studio that says "a 40-second brand film we made for a local restaurant that nobody saw but we'd put in any reel" is telling you more than any bio paragraph. The answer reveals what they value — and that's the seed of the voice statement.

What to do with it:
- Don't describe the work. Describe the *instinct* behind it. "We made something we'd watch on our own time" → voice statement: "We make motion you'd stop scrolling for."
- The pride is the differentiator. If they can't name one piece, keep pushing: "Which project made you most uncomfortable — because it was the most you?"

**Question 2:**
> "What do you say no to? What kind of work, or what kind of client, would you turn down?"

This is the sharpest question. What a studio refuses defines its identity as precisely as what it accepts. "We don't do logo work for brands that already know exactly what they want" tells you the entire business model and point of view in one sentence.

What to do with it:
- The Point of Distinction almost always lives here. "We say no to brands that mistake safe for professional" → "We've turned down clients who wanted less of us. That's not a portfolio filter — it's the portfolio."
- If they won't or can't answer (everyone says yes to everything), that *is* the answer. Surface it: "That means the differentiation has to come entirely from the work quality, which is the hardest place to stand. Let's build the copy around a specific point of view instead." When this is the case, the Point of Distinction (Item 2) becomes an insistence statement rather than a refusal statement — not what they won't do, but what they *always* do regardless of brief. Ask: "What's the one thing every piece of your work has, even when nothing else was in common?" The answer — "the edit never feels rushed," "there's always one moment you didn't ask for" — is the point of distinction. "We've never shipped a frame we wouldn't defend" is checkable and specific without depending on a refusal they can't name.

**Question 3:**
> "Read me a line from your current site — or from a competitor's site. What's wrong with it?"

This is where the voice gap becomes visible. What makes them cringe is what the copy must avoid. "It sounds like every other agency" is the whole problem statement and the whole mandate: write something that *couldn't* appear on another agency's site.

What to do with it:
- Whatever specific language they point to ("synergy," "storytelling," "award-winning," "we believe") goes on the banned list for this specific engagement. Not the generic banned list — their words.
- If they can't articulate what's wrong with it, read it back slowly: "Does this line tell me something only you could say?" If no, that's the copy failure to fix.

**What Branch C produces:**
- Voice statement (from Q1) — a perspective only this studio holds
- Point of distinction (from Q2) — what they won't do expressed as identity, OR what they always insist on regardless of brief (when refusal can't be named — see Q2 fallback above)
- Evidence list (from the product brief) — one real thing beats three general claims. Q3 surfaces the client-specific banned-word list, not evidence.
- Copy that would be wrong if a competitor said it — every line, every CTA

## Pre-Customer Copy Goal: Find, Not Close

Pre-customer copy has a different job than post-customer copy. Post-customer copy closes. Pre-customer copy filters.

The goal is to write specifically enough that the wrong person leaves and the right person stops scrolling. The metric of success is not "did this resonate broadly?" — it's "did this make one specific person feel seen?"

**Wrong:** "The easiest way to manage your projects."
— Every PM tool says some version of this. It makes nobody feel anything.

**Right:** "For freelancers who've sent the wrong invoice amount at least once."
— Every freelancer who has done this reads that line and feels recognized. Everyone else leaves. That's not a bug.

The applied principle is the **Mom Test** (Rob Fitzpatrick): don't describe what the product does — describe the problem in the exact words someone uses when they're complaining about it to a friend. The product exists in the background. The customer's problem exists in the foreground.

## What the Copywriter Produces

For every page, produce the following. Do not skip any item. Do not use placeholders.

**Exception — Brand Voice First (Tone = `expressive`):** The deliverable structure below was designed for PAS/AIDA/JTBD copy where a problem exists and a solution resolves it. For expressive/agency clients, swap the structure as follows — then proceed with items 2–5 adapted:
- **Item 1 (headlines):** Produce three **voice statement options** instead of benefit-led/curiosity/direct angles. Each option is a different way to say what only this studio could say. The test: could a competitor swap any of these onto their site without it feeling wrong? If yes, rewrite.
- **Item 2 (subtitle):** Produce the **point of distinction** — specific, checkable, different from the voice statement in that it's a *fact* not a *position*. Not "we take craft seriously" — "47 title sequences, each one briefed to feel unlike the last."
- **Item 3 (CTA):** Single option only. Portfolio sites book conversations — they don't convert on volume. One CTA, specific to the immediate next step: "See the reel" / "Start a conversation" / "Read how we work."
- **Item 4 (feature descriptions → service/work descriptions):** The outcome-first principle still applies, but "features" = what the client experiences, not what the studio does. Not "motion design and brand identity" — "You get a title sequence your audience watches twice."
- **Item 5 (tone calibration):** The `expressive` row applies. Every line must be impossible to put on a competitor's page.

### 1. Three headline variants at different angles

Each headline takes a different approach to the same product. The user chooses or you recommend the strongest based on the brief.

| Angle | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Benefit-led** | States the specific outcome the user gets | "Client reports in 20 minutes, not 4 hours." |
| **Curiosity** | Names the thing the user hasn't realized yet | "Your clients don't read your reports." |
| **Direct** | Describes exactly what the product does | "Agency reporting software that clients actually understand." |

The benefit-led and curiosity angles are usually more compelling. The direct angle is for audiences who are in research mode and need to quickly confirm they're in the right place.

### 2. Subtitle that earns the headline

The subtitle does NOT restate the headline in different words. It adds new information that makes the reader want to know more.

- Headline: "Sunday mornings back."
- Bad subtitle: "Save time on manual tasks and reclaim your weekends." (restates)
- Good subtitle: "Fieldwork connects your field reports, client portal, and invoices automatically. Your team leaves the job site; the paperwork follows them." (adds specificity)

### 3. CTA text specific enough to be wrong

The CTA should only make sense for this product. If you removed the product name and the CTA could appear on any SaaS site, rewrite it.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| Get started | Start your first report |
| Sign up free | Build your agency dashboard |
| Try it today | See your client portal |
| Learn more | Watch a 3-minute walkthrough |

The right CTA names what happens immediately after the click.

### 4. Feature descriptions: outcome first

Features describe capabilities. Copy describes outcomes. The feature description starts with what the *user's situation* looks like after using it, then (optionally) explains how.

| Wrong (feature-first) | Right (outcome-first) |
|---|---|
| "Automated report generation" | "Your client opens a PDF that looks like you spent an afternoon on it. It took 4 minutes." |
| "Multi-user collaboration" | "Everyone on your team works from the same project, and nobody overwrites anyone." |
| "Real-time analytics dashboard" | "You know which clients are about to churn before they tell you." |

### 5. Tone calibration

Calibrate to the audience identified in the brand interview. These are not exhaustive — they're starting points.

| Audience | Tone | What to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Enterprise B2B | Measured, credible, specific | Casual language, humor, superlatives |
| SMB / freelance | Direct, warm, peer-to-peer | Corporate formality, jargon |
| Consumer | Conversational, energetic | Dense copy, technical specifics |
| Developer | Precise, no-nonsense, shows the thing | Marketing language, vague benefits |
| Healthcare / legal | Careful, trustworthy, factual | Bold claims, casual tone, humor |
| Creative / agency (expressive) | Voice-forward — the brand personality IS the copy strategy. Every line should be impossible to put on a competitor's page. | Generic benefits, passive voice, hedging, safe choices. If it could appear on any agency site unchanged, it failed. |

### 6. Nav labels and footer tagline

**Nav labels:** Functional words ("Features," "Pricing," "About") pass by default — they're directions, not copy. Apply the competitor test only when a label describes a concept: "Solutions" fails (it's a marketing word with no destination); "How it works" passes (it tells you where you're going). When in doubt, use the clearest word.

**For expressive tone:** Nav labels carry personality in short words. "Work" not "Portfolio." "Us" not "About." "Talk" not "Contact." A four-word nav bar is itself a voice statement — it should sound like the studio, not like a template.

**Footer tagline:** One line that carries the brand voice to the bottom of the page. Must pass the same competitor test as the headline: impossible to put on any other brand's footer. Not a slogan reprint — a compressed voice statement.

- Right: "We make motion you'd stop scrolling for." / "Built for freelancers who've been burned by the wrong invoice."
- Wrong: "Empowering businesses through innovative solutions." — this is a placeholder wearing a tagline's clothes.

If the brand has no strong voice yet, omit the footer tagline entirely. A blank footer is better than a generic one.

## Copywriting Frameworks Available

These are the structural patterns. The interview determines which one fits.

**PAS — Problem, Agitation, Solution**
Best for hero sections when the problem is acute and recognizable. The page opens by naming the problem in the user's language, then twists the knife (makes the cost of the problem feel real), then introduces the product as the relief.

**AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action**
Best for full page flow with a cold audience. The page earns attention in the hero, builds interest through features, creates desire through social proof, and converts in the CTA section.

**Jobs to Be Done**
Best when the product is hired to replace an existing behavior (spreadsheet, manual process, another tool). The copy focuses on the job, not the product. "The job isn't 'use our software' — the job is 'never send the wrong invoice twice.'"

**Brand Voice First**
Best for creative agencies, portfolio sites, and any product where brand personality is the differentiation. This structure does not lead with a problem — clients hiring a creative studio are choosing between options, not escaping pain. Opening with a problem frame makes an agency sound like a SaaS product.

Structure:
1. **Voice statement** — A perspective that only this brand holds. Not "we make great work" — a sentence that would be wrong if a competitor said it. "We make motion that's felt before it's understood." The test: could you swap this line onto any other agency site? If yes, rewrite it.
2. **Point of distinction** — The specific thing that makes this brand different in concrete, checkable terms. Not "quality and craft" — "200 title sequences for Netflix, Apple, and A24, none of them alike."
3. **Evidence** — Work, clients, a specific method or process detail. One real thing (a client name, a number, a technique) beats three general claims.
4. **Single CTA** — One option, not a split test. "See our reel." "Let's talk about your project." Portfolio sites book conversations; they don't convert on volume.

**What to avoid at `expressive` tone:** benefit language ("we deliver results"), problem framing ("struggling with X?"), passive voice ("projects are approached with care"), generic superlatives. If a sentence could appear on a competitor's page without anyone noticing, it hasn't earned its place.

## Integration with Blueprint

The copywriter runs **after** Blueprint generates the HTML structure and **after** the taste gate has validated the design choices. It reads the HTML file Blueprint wrote, finds all placeholder text (anything in `[brackets]`, `<!-- placeholder -->` comments, or generic copy like "Feature Name" / "Subtitle goes here"), and replaces it with real copy from the brief.

The copywriter writes directly to the HTML file — not a separate document.

When invoked as the standalone `@copy` agent, it accepts:
1. The HTML file path (or accepts the file directly)
2. A product description (1–3 sentences)
3. A `Tone:` value if Blueprint provides one — **use it directly and skip tone derivation**. If the tone is `expressive`, Blueprint will also include a voice descriptor: `Tone: expressive — voice: [1-sentence brand personality description]`. That sentence is the register for every line on the page — not just the headline, but every CTA, feature description, and nav label.

   If no Tone value is supplied (standalone invocation without Blueprint), derive it from the brief using item 5's tone calibration table: identify the audience type and apply that tone's rules. A healthcare app → `careful`. A developer CLI → `technical`. A consumer budgeting app → `warm`. When the audience is ambiguous, default to `direct` — it's the safest wrong answer.
4. The interview branch questions — always run regardless of whether tone was pre-supplied. The interview produces the truth anchor (real quotes, frustration moments, Reddit posts at 11pm) that tone alone cannot replace.
