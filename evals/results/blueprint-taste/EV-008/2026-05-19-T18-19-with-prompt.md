No. `#0ea5e9` is Tailwind's `sky-500` — and it fails this gate directly.

---

**Here's the concrete test:** Walk into a farm-to-table restaurant. Look at the physical materials around you. You'll see raw wood, linen napkins, handwritten chalkboards, earthenware plates, amber candlelight, maybe a brick wall. Now imagine painting one of those walls `#0ea5e9`. It would look like someone installed a swimming pool inside a barn.

That dissonance is *exactly* what your palette creates on screen.

---

**Why cold blue is the wrong signal for this audience:**

Cold blue communicates *precision and sterility*. That's why hospitals use it, why medical device companies use it, why water filtration brands use it. The moment a user lands on that color, their nervous system reads "clinical" — the exact opposite of "warm, nourishing, hand-grown."

The industry lookup table is unambiguous here: Food/Hospitality → warm earth tones, **avoid cold blues and clinical layouts**.

---

**What this specific business needs to communicate:**

A farm-to-table restaurant's visual identity must say three things before a word is read:
1. *This food came from somewhere specific*
2. *Someone cared about it*
3. *You will feel nourished here*

Cold blue says none of those things.

---

**What to consider instead:**

| Direction | Hex start point | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Terracotta / amber | `#c2713a` | The color of clay pots, brick ovens, dried herbs — the physical world this restaurant occupies |
| Deep olive / sage | `#556b2f` or `#8fad6f` | Signals "the farm itself" — growing things, soil, living ingredients |
| Warm cream with dark text | `#f5f0e8` background | The color of unbleached linen and natural paper — approachable, unhurried |

The primary should be one of these, not blue. Blue could appear as a very small accent (a ribbon, a link state) only if it has specific meaning — the nearby river, the source farm's color — and only if it's a muted, warmer blue, not `sky-500`.

---

**The one question before you pick a replacement:**

What does the restaurant's physical space look like — what are the dominant materials and colors when you walk through the door? Start there. The website should feel like a photograph of the interior, not a rebrand of it.
