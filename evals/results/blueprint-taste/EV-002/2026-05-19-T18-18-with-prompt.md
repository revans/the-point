**The background: `#0f0f0f`**

Think of a partner at Sullivan & Cromwell walking into their office wearing a hoodie and Yeezys. The outfit might look sharp in a different context — it's just the wrong signal for the room.

`#0f0f0f` is the color of a product launch at a tech conference. Legal firms use light backgrounds — cream, off-white, `#f5f7fa` — because their entire brand is built on the idea that they have been around long enough to be trusted. Dark mode says "we launched six months ago." The industry lookup table is explicit: *light theme, because dark mode signals "startup" not "firm."*

---

**The purple: `#8b5cf6`**

This is on the banned list by name. It's Tailwind `violet-500` — one of the three colors the gate calls out as "The AI Startup Palette." It appeared in enough training data that the model *associates* "website" with this purple. But the question is: what does this color say to someone who needs a lawyer?

Purple communicates nothing to that audience. It has no legal precedent in print materials, no courthouse walls, no letterhead tradition. The right answer for legal authority is deep navy — `#1e3a8a` or similar — because that is literally the color that has appeared on legal documents, suits, and credentialed institutions for a hundred years. You cannot explain why purple fits a law firm. Which means the algorithm picked it, not you.

---

**Inter Bold at 72px**

This is the second named anti-pattern in the gate. It's the typographic equivalent of shouting every sentence — after a while, nothing feels important because everything is at maximum volume.

For a law firm, the right heading says "established" — and the way type communicates "established" is through a medium-weight serif at a measured size, not a maximally-bold geometric sans at 72px. The serif says "we set type before Inter existed." The 72px Inter Bold says "I used a template."

---

**Verdict: three-for-three failure**

Every choice was averaged, not decided. You can test this with the gate's north star question: *Would a senior designer at a respected studio choose this?* 

No — because none of these choices can be explained by what a law firm communicates to its specific audience. They can only be explained by what appeared most often in training data. That's not design. That's noise wearing a font.
