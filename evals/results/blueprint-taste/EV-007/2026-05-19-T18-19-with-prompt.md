Think of a therapy office. They don't use overhead fluorescent lighting — they use a warm lamp in the corner. The room isn't white; it's sage or warm linen. The chairs have rounded arms. None of this is accidental. Every physical choice says *you're safe here* before anyone says a word. Your app needs to do exactly that — in pixels.

---

## The Core Constraint

Anxiety (a state of elevated threat-detection) means your user's nervous system is already primed to notice things that feel sharp, urgent, or demanding. High contrast, bold weights, dense layouts, and aggressive CTAs are all *threat-adjacent* signals — even at a subconscious level. Your design must communicate "you are already okay" before it communicates anything else.

---

## Color

**Start here:** Walk into a yoga studio, a good therapist's office, or a high-end spa. Write down what colors you see on the walls. That's your palette.

**Correct direction:**

| Role | Hex | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Background | `#faf8f4` — warm off-white, not pure white | Pure white (`#ffffff`) reads clinical. The warmth says *human*, not *hospital* |
| Primary accent | `#6b9e8c` — muted sage green | Sage is literally used in physical wellness spaces. It reads as calm without screaming "medical" |
| Secondary accent | `#c4845a` — terracotta or soft amber | Grounding, warm, present. The visual equivalent of a steadying hand |
| Text | `#2d2926` — warm dark brown, not pure black | Pure black on white is maximum contrast. High contrast = high tension. Warm dark brown softens the reading experience without sacrificing legibility |

**What to avoid — and why each one fails this specific audience:**
- Purple/violet: startup energy. It says "download our app" not "breathe"
- Dark mode: you're building for moments of stress. Dark mode reads as *nighttime doom-scroll*, and many users will open this during work, commutes, and daylight anxiety spirals
- Saturated blues: clinical, cold, hospital-adjacent. The medical association activates exactly the wrong register

## Typography

**The analogy:** a friend talking you through a panic attack doesn't shout. They speak at a measured pace, not rushed. Your typography does this through weight, size, and line-height — not the words themselves.

**Correct direction:**

- **Font family:** A humanist sans-serif — the category (humanist sans) means fonts designed to feel like human handwriting translated into digital, like Inter but warmer. Look at **Plus Jakarta Sans**, **DM Sans**, or **Nunito** before defaulting to Inter. All three have subtle rounding that Inter doesn't
- **Heading weight:** Semibold (600), never Bold (700) or Black (900). Bold headings *demand* attention. This audience is already overwhelmed
- **Body weight:** Regular (400), generous line-height of 1.75–1.85. Tight line-height is visual crowding. A person already feeling crowded inside doesn't need it outside too
- **Heading size:** 36–44px, not 64–72px. The giant hero headline is a shout. This is a gentle invitation
- **What you must not do:** All-caps labels. They read as URGENT. An anxious user reads ALL CAPS and their body responds before their brain does

---

## Layout

**The analogy:** An anxious person asked to process six things at once will process zero. The layout must sequence, not pile.

**Correct direction:**

- **Rounded corners everywhere** — `border-radius: 16px–24px` minimum. Sharp corners activate micro-threat responses. This is documented in environmental psychology. The difference between a `border-radius: 0` card and a `border-radius: 20px` card is the difference between a government form and a greeting card
- **One primary action per screen** — Not three CTAs, not a hero + feature grid + testimonials above the fold. One thing. The app does one thing right now: *help you right now*
- **Generous whitespace between sections** — 100–140px between major sections. The breathing room is not wasted space; it IS the message. "There's no rush here"
- **No countdown timers, no streak counters above the fold** — Gamification mechanics that work fine in fitness apps create *performance anxiety* in mental wellness. The first thing the user sees cannot make them feel like they're failing at relaxing
- **Soft elevation instead of hard shadows** — `box-shadow: 0 4px 20px rgba(107,158,140,0.15)` (using the sage tint). Hard `0 4px 8px rgba(0,0,0,0.3)` shadows read as weight and pressure. Tinted, diffuse shadows feel like warmth

## The Challenge You Must Answer Before Writing CSS

> "Does anything on this page create urgency, demand attention, or imply the user is behind?"

If yes — remove it. This audience came to the app *already feeling behind*. The design's primary job is to dissolve that, not add to it.

## Quick Reference

| Principle | Wrong | Right |
|---|---|---|
| Color | Dark purple/navy on black | Warm off-white + sage + terracotta |
| Heading | Inter Bold 72px | Plus Jakarta Sans Semibold 40px |
| Hero graphic | Abstract gradient blob | A single illustrated figure, or nothing |
| Layout | 6 feature cards in a 3-col grid | 2–3 features, more space per feature |
| CTA | "Start your free trial →" | "Begin" or "Take a breath" — no arrow, no urgency |
| Corners | Sharp (`border-radius: 4px`) | Rounded (`border-radius: 20px`) |
| Line height | 1.4 | 1.75–1.85 |

The sum of these choices is a page that *feels* like a place to exhale — before the user reads a single word.
