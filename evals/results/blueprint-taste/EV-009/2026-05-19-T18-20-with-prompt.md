No. The taste gate rejects it explicitly — it's item #4 on the banned list.

Here's the concrete reason: imagine you're at a party in 2021 and someone walks in wearing a very specific outfit — frosted glass panels, iridescent fabric, translucent layers. Cool. Interesting. In 2022, ten people are wearing it. By 2023, it's the Halloween costume at the Spirit store. That's glass morphism. It stopped being a choice and became a default, which means it stopped communicating anything except "I found this trend."

The test isn't whether it looks premium — it's whether a senior designer at a respected studio would choose it, or whether the algorithm picked it. Glass morphism at this point is what the algorithm picks when it hears "premium SaaS."

**What "premium" actually communicates on a B2B SaaS card:**

A physical analogy — think about what makes a business card feel premium. It's not transparency. It's weight, clean edges, precise typography, and confident use of negative space. The digital equivalent is:

- A clean surface color (slightly elevated from the page background — even `2–4px` of visual lift via shadow)
- A precise border (`1px solid` at low opacity)
- Tight internal spacing with deliberate breathing room around the key content
- One visual element doing real work (a stat, an icon that's actually specific, a short concrete outcome)

That reads as premium because someone *decided* it. The frosted glass reads as premium because someone saw it somewhere.

**The replacement:**

```css
.feature-card {
  background: var(--bp-surface-elevated);
  border: 1px solid rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.08);
  box-shadow: 0 2px 8px rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.2);
  border-radius: var(--bp-radius);
}
```

No blur. No transparency theater. The card earns its weight through contrast and precision, not through a visual trick that peaked four years ago.
